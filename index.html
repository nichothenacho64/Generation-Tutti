Generation Tutti

Exploring everyday conversations across Italy, across generations

What don't we know?

Despite widespread awareness of the idea that languages change over time, we lack a clear, data-driven understanding of how everyday conversation really differs between generations in Italy. This is not just in terms of vocabulary, but also tone, sentiment, and dialectal variation, where we explore how Italian not only changes as a spoken language but as a form of expression for many Italians. So you might ponder on questions like the following:

Do younger and older generations express themselves in the same way?
How much of the Italian language is shared across generations, and how much is changing with times?
Is regional language/dialect use really declining, or is its use just changing?

Here's the problem: Aspects like these are rarely visualised or compared in a systematic way, especially using real, organic, everyday conversational data. There is no accessible, interactive way to see and hear how language evolves from one generation to the next.

Significance

Languages are constantly evolving thanks to many factors from techological to social changes. Such changes are are especially sophisticated due to linguistic diversity, the cultural value of speech, changes in self-expression, etc. Previous studies have examined linguistic change in an academic context, though the everyday spoken language (i.e. how we speak in informal quotidian contexts) remains underexplored, especially across different generations. Hence, in our research, we aim to compare and contrast the nature of everyday conversations in Italy across generations.

The researchers at KIParla define free conversations as those that have spontaneous conversational speech among people all across the country (Mauri et al., 2022), without any instructions from the research. Accordingly, we define ‘everyday conversations’ as those that have no thematic alignment and unfold organically in recreational settings such as meals. However, based on widespread consensus as well as new Gen Z analysis by the Pew Research Center, and the one generation defined by the U.S. Census Bureau (Baby Boomers), these are the birth years and ages of the generations you’ll want to use in 2025. We have drawn on the Pew Research Center’s definitions for the age range of each generation, given their widespread consensus in the USA (Dimock, 2018). Though there are specific definitions like these, we have had to accommodate for the fact that each participant had an age range rather than a specific age; hence, our definitions may differ slightly.
