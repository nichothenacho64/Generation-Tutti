Generation Tutti

Exploring everyday conversations across Italy, across generations

What don't we know?

Despite widespread awareness of the idea that languages change over time, we lack a clear, data-driven understanding of how everyday conversation really differs between generations in Italy. This is not just in terms of vocabulary, but also tone, sentiment, and dialectal variation, where we explore how Italian not only changes as a spoken language but as a form of expression for many Italians. So you might ponder on questions like the following:

Do younger and older generations express themselves in the same way?
How much of the Italian language is shared across generations, and how much is changing with times?
Is regional language/dialect use really declining, or is its use just changing?

Here's the problem: Aspects like these are rarely visualised or compared in a systematic way, especially using real, organic, everyday conversational data. There is no accessible, interactive way to see and hear how language evolves from one generation to the next.

Significance

Languages are constantly evolving thanks to many factors from techological to social changes. Such changes are are especially sophisticated due to linguistic diversity, the cultural value of speech, changes in self-expression, etc. Previous studies have examined linguistic change in an academic context, though the everyday spoken language (i.e. how we speak in informal quotidian contexts) remains underexplored, especially across different generations. Hence, in our research, we aim to compare and contrast the nature of everyday conversations in Italy across generations.

The researchers at KIParla define free conversations as those that have spontaneous conversational speech among people all across the country (Mauri et al., 2022), without any instructions from the research. Accordingly, we define ‘everyday conversations’ as those that have no thematic alignment and unfold organically in recreational settings such as meals. However, based on widespread consensus as well as new Gen Z analysis by the Pew Research Center, and the one generation defined by the U.S. Census Bureau (Baby Boomers), these are the birth years and ages of the generations you’ll want to use in 2025. We have drawn on the Pew Research Center’s definitions for the age range of each generation, given their widespread consensus in the USA (Dimock, 2018). Though there are specific definitions like these, we have had to accommodate for the fact that each participant had an age range rather than a specific age; hence, our definitions may differ slightly.

Emotional tone

A Generational Mood Swing: From Negativity and Less Expression to Positivity and More Expression

How do you think your parents' conversations sound compared to your own? Our data reveals a significant emotional divide. The chart below shows the sentiment of everyday conversations for the four generations of Italians examined.

While Baby Boomer and Generation X conversations are predominantly negative (46.3% and 50% respectively), Generation Y and Generation Z have completely gone the other way, recording positive sentiment both at 47.5%. This is not merely about what they feel, but how they express it. The way that generations use their voices through their pitch, volume, and pace also shares a compelling story.

How does linguistic change sound?

In the chart above, we can see that younger Italians speak with more animation. Their more frequent use of raised volume and rising intonation (normal and weak) is significantly higher than that of older generations. Baby Boomers, instead have more similar levels of low volume and slowing down, perhaps indicative of their more reserved conversational style. This reveals two distinctive conversational styles: one that is lively and dynamic, and another that is more calm and measured.

Lexical and thematic foundations

Do us Italians still speak the same language?

Beyond the emotional tone, what about the words themselves? We analysed many lemmas (i.e. the base form of words, excluding those with fewer than 3 letters) to see the vocabulary that unites and divides the generations.

At first glance, a common conversational foundation is apparent. Basic perceptive and communicative verbs like vedere (to see) parlare (to speak) sentire (to hear/feel) are common across all generations. However, the linguistic story takes a sharp turn with Generation Z when we examine the delta change in generations. Their use of many common verbs drops significantly. For example, the frequency of prendere (to take) drops from a high of 19.9 for Baby Boomers to just 0.6 for Generation Z. Furthermore, verbs like andare (to go) and mangiare (to eat) have illustrated an increase, highlighting the importance of everyday important tasks in their conversations. This change in vocabulary additionally reflects a shift in what generations talk about.

From deeper discussions to casual chats

Older generations gravitate towards discussions; meanwhile, younger generations tend to prefer lighter, more casual conversation pieces. For these Italians, it seems that the very purpose of everyday conversations is changing, from discussions towards more informal connection and sharing.

Regional identity

The surprising truth about Italian dialects/regional languages

We've all heard the standard narrative that local dialects are disappearing in favour of standard Italian. However, our data reveals a more fascinating and nuanced reality.

This map uncovers some of the most unexpected findings in the project. While dialect usage is declining in parts of the North (in grey areas, particularly Veneto, with a 14.9% decrease), it is making a significant comeback in the South, in particular, in Puglia (in the red area). Instead of merely being a thing of the past, dialect usage in Puglia appears to be a growing part of modern identity for younger generations, directly challenging the idea of a uniform decline in dialect usage. This trend is not only about geography. A closer look reveals that where people live, their generation, and educational background strongly shape the usage of dialect in their conversations.

As the charts illustrate, dialect use has some relationship to a participant's educational background and macro region. When examining each conversation, those with people from an elementary or middle school education tend to have a higher frequency of dialect words, though this does not show a significant correlation with the average age of participants. As seen previously, southern conversations show a steady increase in the usage of dialect. This once again proves that dialect is not just a remnant of the past, but a living, evolving feature of modern Italian identity.

Conclusion: One country, many voices

Our journey through everyday conversations all across Italy demonstrate Italy as a linguistically transitioning nation. We've witnessed a profound emotional shift, with younger generations bringing a wave of positivity and expression to their chats. We've uncovered lexical divergences, as Generation Z redefines everyday conversation with vocabulary changes.

Most surprisingly, we've debunked the myth of disappearing dialects across the country. In its place, we find a regional alignment where dialect is not just surviving, but thriving in the South, re-emerging as a marker of local and regional identity.

The data found highlights a fascinating tension between national linguistic standards and perceptions and the persistent power of regional and generational identity. There is no single 'Italian way' of speaking, but many. Whilst the data analysed in this project is not super extensive, it nonetheless postulates the idea that the Italian language is a vibrant, living organism that reflects our emotions, our culture, and our identity and place in a rapidly changing world.
